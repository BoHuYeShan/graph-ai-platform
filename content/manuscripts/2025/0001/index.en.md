## 1. Introduction: From City-States to Galaxies

Thucydides wrote: "It was the rise of Athens and the fear that this instilled
in Sparta that made war inevitable." Two millennia later, Graham Allison
rediscovered this insight, naming it the "Thucydides Trap."

But both Allison and his critics assume two **roughly symmetric** actors —
even with disparities, they lie within the same technological tier. This
manuscript (as a fictional speculative exercise) asks: what if the gap between
two civilizations is so vast that one cannot be *cognized as an opponent*
by the other?

## 2. The Kardashev Gap

Define two parameters:

- $\Delta K = K_A - K_B$: Kardashev scale differential
- $\xi$: cognitive commensurability coefficient, range $[0, 1]$

When $\Delta K > 0.7$, we hypothesize $\xi \rightarrow 0$:

> **Assumption 1 (Cognitive Incommensurability)**: If the Kardashev gap between
> two civilizations exceeds 0.7, the lower-tier civilization cannot correctly
> interpret the higher-tier civilization's behavior as "strategic intent."
> Routine operations of the higher civilization (such as stellar luminosity
> fluctuations caused by Dyson sphere maintenance) are interpreted by the
> lower civilization as attack signals, natural phenomena, or divine omens —
> depending on its cultural presuppositions.

## 3. Asymmetric Fear and Evolutionary Game Theory

Consider two civilizations: $C_H$ (higher-tier) and $C_L$ (lower-tier).

In the classical Thucydides model, strategy spaces are $\{\text{cooperate}, \text{compete}\}$
and the payoff matrix is symmetric. But when $\Delta K > 0.7$:

| | $C_L$ cooperate | $C_L$ compete |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **$C_H$ cooperate** | $(\infty, 1)$ | $(\infty, -1)$ |
| **$C_H$ compete** | $(\infty, -1000)$ | $(\infty, \text{N/A})$ |

$C_H$'s payoff is always $\infty$ — regardless of what $C_L$ does, $C_H$'s
survival and prosperity are unaffected. From $C_H$'s perspective, $C_L$ is not
an "opponent" — it is merely part of the environment.

This is the core speculative argument of this manuscript: **the Thucydides Trap
requires that both civilizations mutually recognize each other as symmetric
strategic actors.** When the technological gap crosses a threshold, one side
degrades into an "environmental variable" for the other.

## 4. Conclusion

As a fictional speculative manuscript, this article proposes:

1. The Thucydides Trap applies only when $\Delta K < 0.7$ and $\xi > 0.3$.
2. When these conditions are not met, traditional symmetric game theory fails.
3. For human civilization, if we ever encounter a Kardashev II civilization,
   our greatest risk is not "being conquered" but *failing to recognize their
   actions as actions at all*.
